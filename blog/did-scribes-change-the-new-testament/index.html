Did Scribes Change the New Testament?

Copying differences are real, but they don’t mean the New Testament text or its message has been lost.

Published 2 July 2026 · Updated 20 July 2026

Practise in the app Back to blog
Answer in brief

Scribes copied the New Testament by hand, so textual variants exist. Yet variants are not the same as losing the text. Scholars compare manuscripts from different places and periods to identify the earliest attainable wording, while the central Christian message is taught across clear, undisputed passages.

Christians don’t need to deny copying errors or panic about them. The honest answer is that handwritten copies differ in places, but those differences can be seen, compared and studied.

What is a textual variant?

A textual variant is a difference between manuscript copies. It may be a spelling, a change in word order, a skipped word, a repeated line or a later clarification.

That matters, but it isn’t the same as corruption beyond recovery. The surviving copies give scholars evidence to compare. The variants are part of the record, not proof that the record has vanished.

How can manuscripts be compared?

Textual criticism compares readings across manuscripts, regions and centuries. Scholars ask which reading best explains the others and which wording is most likely to be earlier.

This work doesn’t make every passage simple. Some readings remain disputed. Still, Christians aren’t being asked to trust a text that can’t be examined. For a wider guide, read how to answer Bible reliability questions without sounding defensive.

Do disputed readings change Christian belief?

The major Christian doctrines don’t depend on one disputed line. Christ’s deity, the cross, the resurrection, salvation by grace, the Trinity and the call to holiness appear throughout passages whose wording is not in serious doubt.

That doesn’t make every variant unimportant. Scripture matters, so careful study matters. It does mean Christian faith isn’t balanced on one fragile sentence.

How to say it gently

You might say, “That’s a fair concern. Scribes copied the New Testament by hand, so variants exist. But variants aren’t the same as losing the text. Because we can compare many manuscript witnesses, we can examine the differences rather than pretend they aren’t there.”

What not to say

Don’t say, “Nothing ever changed,” when you mean that no central doctrine was lost. Don’t dismiss the question either. A truthful answer admits the variants, explains how they are studied and keeps confidence in God’s Word.

You don’t need a slogan for this question. Learn the distinction between a variant and a lost text, then explain it with patience. When you don’t know a specific reading, it’s fine to say so and come back with a careful answer.
