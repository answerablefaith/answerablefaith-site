Did Jesus Say “I Am God; Worship Me”?

Jesus did not use that exact modern sentence, but He claimed divine identity through His names, works, authority, honour and glory.

Published 20 July 2026 · Updated 20 July 2026

Practise in the app Back to blog
Answer in brief

Jesus did not use the exact modern sentence “I am God; worship me.” Yet He claimed divine identity by forgiving sins, using God’s “I am” language, sharing the Father’s honour and glory, and receiving worship. His hearers understood the force of those claims, and the New Testament presents Him as truly divine.

Did Jesus claim to be God? Yes, though He did not express the claim in the exact words many people now demand. The better question is whether Jesus said and did things that would be blasphemous for a mere prophet.

Why didn’t Jesus simply say, “I am God”?

Modern readers often expect a short formula shaped by a modern debate. Jesus spoke within the language of Israel’s Scriptures. He revealed His identity through divine names, actions, honour and authority. That was no less clear to His first hearers. They accused Him of blasphemy or tried to stone Him because they understood the claim.

We should still be precise. Jesus is not the Father, and Christianity does not teach that there are two gods. The Nicene Creed confesses the Son as “true God from true God” and of one being with the Father. The claim is that the eternal Son shares the one divine nature while remaining personally distinct from the Father.

Where did Jesus use God’s divine name?

In John 8:58, Jesus says, “Before Abraham was, I am.” He does not merely claim to be older than Abraham. His wording echoes God’s self-revelation in Exodus 3:14 and the “I am” language of Isaiah. The crowd responds by picking up stones. John wants readers to hear a claim about divine identity and eternal existence.

The same Gospel opens by saying that the Word was with God and was God, then identifies the Word as the One who became flesh (John 1:1, 14). Jesus’ own statement fits that frame. He existed before Abraham because His life did not begin at Bethlehem.

Where did Jesus claim God’s authority?

Mark 2:5–12 records Jesus forgiving a paralysed man’s sins. The scribes ask, “Who can forgive sins but God alone?” They understood that sin is finally committed against God. Jesus then heals the man to show that “the Son of Man has authority on earth to forgive sins.” He does not merely announce forgiveness as a prophet. He grants it in His own authority.

Jesus also calls Himself Lord of the Sabbath (Mark 2:28). The Sabbath belonged to God’s covenant rule, yet Jesus places it under His authority. In Mark 14:61–64, He joins Psalm 110:1 and Daniel 7:13–14, saying that the Son of Man will sit at the right hand of Power and come with the clouds of heaven. The high priest answers with a charge of blasphemy.

Where did Jesus claim equal honour with the Father?

John 5:18 says Jesus’ opponents understood Him to be “making himself equal with God”. Jesus does not correct them by saying they have misunderstood. He goes on to claim that the Son gives life, executes final judgement and must be honoured “just as” the Father is honoured (John 5:21–23). No faithful creature may demand the same honour as God.

In John 10:30, Jesus says, “I and the Father are one.” The surrounding verses concern His power to give eternal life and keep His sheep secure. His opponents reply that He, “being a man”, makes Himself God (John 10:33). Jesus distinguishes Himself from the Father, yet claims a unity of divine power that explains their reaction.

Did Jesus receive worship and divine titles?

Jesus receives worship from His disciples after walking on the sea (Matthew 14:33) and after His resurrection (Matthew 28:9, 17). The strongest scene comes in John 20:28, when Thomas says to Him, “My Lord and my God!” Jesus does not rebuke Thomas. He blesses those who will believe the same truth without seeing Him in the flesh.

Jesus also speaks of the glory He shared with the Father “before the world existed” (John 17:5). Prophets receive a task from God. Jesus speaks as the eternal Son who shared divine glory before creation and who perfectly reveals the Father. “Whoever has seen me has seen the Father,” He tells Philip (John 14:9).

Does this depend only on John’s Gospel?

No. John states Jesus’ divine identity with unusual directness, but the same pattern appears across the Gospels. In Mark, Jesus forgives sins, rules the Sabbath, commands creation and identifies Himself with Daniel’s heavenly Son of Man. In Matthew and Luke, He receives worship, speaks as the final judge and places allegiance to Himself above every earthly loyalty.

The evidence is clear and cumulative. One verse may be debated in isolation. Together, the divine name, works, authority, honour, glory and worship form a coherent picture. The New Testament does not present Jesus as a man who later became God. It presents the eternal Son who became man for our salvation.

How to say it gently

You might say: “Jesus didn’t use that exact modern sentence, and I don’t want to pretend every passage is equally direct. But He forgave sins, used God’s ‘I am’ language, claimed equal honour with the Father and received worship. Could we read John 5, John 8 and John 20 together and ask what those claims mean?”

What not to say

Don’t mock the question or answer with a pile of references and no context. Don’t claim that Jesus is the Father, or that Christians believe in several gods. Don’t force one verse to carry the whole case. Show the pattern, explain the Jewish setting and let the passages speak.

A useful next step is to read John 5:16–29, John 8:48–59 and John 20:24–31 in full. Then ask whether Jesus’ words and actions fit a mere prophet, or whether the Gospel writers are calling us to confess Him with Thomas: “My Lord and my God.”
