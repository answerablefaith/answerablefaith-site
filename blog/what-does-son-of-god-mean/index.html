What Does Son of God Mean?

The title names Jesus as the eternal Son, not a created child or a lesser kind of God.

Published 17 July 2026 · Updated 17 July 2026

Practise in the app Back to blog
Answer in brief

“Son of God” doesn’t mean God had a physical child. It means Jesus is the eternal Son who shares the Father’s divine nature, reveals Him perfectly and reigns as the promised King. The title describes an eternal relationship within the one God, not a biological event or a created being.

What does Son of God mean? Christians use the title to say something central about Jesus: He is eternally related to the Father, fully divine and personally distinct from the Father. He didn’t become the Son at Bethlehem. The eternal Son became human there for our salvation.

What does Son of God mean in Christianity?

The word Son describes who Jesus is in relation to the Father. It doesn’t mean that God is male in a bodily sense, that He had a wife or that Jesus came into being through physical reproduction. God is spirit (John 4:24), and Christian teaching rejects every biological picture of divine sonship.

Sonship also tells us that Jesus is more than a messenger from God. Hebrews 1:3 says, “He is the radiance of the glory of God and the exact imprint of his nature.” The Son makes the Father known because He shares the same divine nature. The Father is God, the Son is God, and the Son is not the Father. This is personal distinction without division in the one God.

Does “Son” mean Jesus was created?

No. Human sons begin after their fathers, but we can’t press human biology into God’s eternal life. John 1:1 says, “In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God.” Before creation, the Son already was. John then adds, “All things were made through him” (John 1:3). The One through whom all created things came to be cannot belong to the class of created things.

John 1:14 says that “the Word became flesh”. It doesn’t say the Word began to exist. The Son took a true human nature while remaining who He eternally is. Bethlehem marks the incarnation, not the origin of the Son.

What does “begotten, not made” mean?

The Nicene Creed calls Jesus Christ “the only-begotten Son of God” and says He is “begotten, not made, of one substance with the Father”. That language was chosen to rule out the claim that the Son is God’s highest creature. Made things belong to creation. The Son belongs eternally to the identity of God.

Begotten names the Son’s eternal relation to the Father. It does not describe an event in time, a physical process or a moment when He started. The Westminster Confession of Faith, chapter 8, therefore calls the Son “very and eternal God, of one substance and equal with the Father”. Historic Christian doctrine speaks this carefully because loose language can turn Jesus into either a second god or a lesser being. The Bible allows neither.

Is Son of God only a title for the Messiah?

The title has a royal meaning. In 2 Samuel 7:14, God promises that David’s royal heir will be treated as His son. Psalm 2:7 uses sonship language for God’s anointed king. Jesus is the promised Messiah who fulfils that royal hope and rules with God’s authority.

Yet the New Testament does more than give Jesus an honourable title. The Son existed before His human birth, shares the Father’s glory and does works that belong to God. In John 5:18–23, Jesus’ claim to a unique sonship leads directly to the charge that He was making Himself equal with God. He then says that all should honour the Son just as they honour the Father. His royal sonship rests on His deeper identity as the eternal Son.

How does the Son reveal the Father?

Jesus doesn’t reveal God from a distance. He reveals the Father from within the eternal life of God. When Philip asks to see the Father, Jesus answers, “Whoever has seen me has seen the Father” (John 14:9). He isn’t saying that He and the Father are the same person. He means that the Father’s character, truth and saving purpose are perfectly shown in the Son.

This gives the title pastoral weight. God hasn’t left us to guess what He is like. In Jesus we see holy love, authority over evil, compassion for sinners and the self-giving grace of the cross. The Son makes the unseen Father known and brings believers into fellowship with Him.

How should Christians explain this to Muslim friends?

For many Muslims, Son of God sounds like a claim that God had a physical relationship. Christians should reject that idea clearly rather than acting offended by the question. Start with shared agreement: God has no wife, Jesus isn’t the result of procreation, and divine sonship isn’t bodily.

Then explain the actual difference. Christianity teaches that the one God eternally exists as Father, Son and Holy Spirit. The claim isn’t that a man became divine. It is that the eternal Son took human nature and came among us. Respect doesn’t require hiding the disagreement, and conviction doesn’t require speaking with contempt.

How to say it gently

You could say: “Christians don’t mean that God had a physical child. We mean that Jesus is eternally the Son. He shares the Father’s divine nature, shows us the Father perfectly and came as God’s promised King. His birth in Bethlehem is when He became human, not when He began to exist.”

What not to say

Don’t say, “It’s just a metaphor”, because the title makes a real claim about Jesus’ eternal identity. Don’t say Jesus is a separate god, a created helper or merely a special prophet. Don’t tell someone they’re foolish for hearing the phrase biologically. Correct the misunderstanding, then explain the Christian claim with patience.

The best next step is to read John 1:1–18 and Hebrews 1:1–4 slowly. Ask what these passages say about the Son before creation, His relation to the Father and His work in making God known. Christian faith rests on the person revealed there: the eternal Son who became flesh for us.
