How should Christians answer slavery in the Bible?

Start with moral clarity. Then handle the Bible’s ancient world with care and a tone that does not dodge the pain.

Practise in the app Back to blog
Answer in brief

Christians should plainly condemn race-based chattel slavery as sin. People made in God’s image must not be treated as property. The Bible’s ancient setting needs care, but no Christian should sound like they are defending exploitation.

This is not a question to brush aside. Scripture has been misused to defend great evil. Christians should say that clearly before moving into history or context.

A good answer begins with truth and compassion. The person asking may not be trying to win a point. They may be asking whether Christianity can face a serious moral wound.

Start with moral clarity

The first point should be simple: human beings are made in God’s image. They must not be bought, sold or treated as less than human.

So Christians should not defend racial oppression. We should not excuse kidnapping or abuse. We should not sound annoyed that the question has been asked.

Do not flatten the ancient world

The word “slavery” can cover different things in the Bible’s world. It can include debt-service, household service, war captivity and harsh oppression.

That does not make the question easy. It does mean we should avoid simple claims that treat every text as if it describes the same thing.

Regulation is not approval

Some biblical laws deal with broken social realities. A law that limits harm is not the same as saying the harm is good.

For example, Exodus 21:16 condemns kidnapping and selling a person. That matters. It shows that the Bible does not give people permission to steal humans and sell them.

Keep Scripture’s bigger story in view

The Bible begins with people made in God’s image. It calls rulers and masters to answer to God. It tells Christians that in Christ, believers are brothers and sisters.

Philemon 16 is important here. Paul tells Philemon to receive Onesimus as more than a bondservant, as a beloved brother. The gospel pushes against treating people as mere property.

How to say it gently

You might say, “I agree this is a serious moral question. Christians should condemn chattel slavery clearly. These texts need careful handling, but they do not give anyone permission to treat people as property.”

Then keep listening. A careful answer should never sound cold.

What not to say

Do not say, “It was not that bad.” Do not rush past the moral concern. Do not act as if a historical distinction removes every hard question.

Also do not give a neat answer that makes the problem feel small. This topic deserves moral seriousness.

FAQ

Should Christians condemn chattel slavery?

Yes. Christians should plainly condemn race-based chattel slavery as sin.

Does regulation mean approval?

No. A law that restrains evil in a fallen world is not the same as calling that evil good.

Why does tone matter here?

Because people may be raising a real moral wound, not just an argument. A careless answer can do harm.

What should Christians say first?

Start with this: people made in God’s image must not be treated as property.
