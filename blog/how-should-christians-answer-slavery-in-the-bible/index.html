How should Christians answer slavery in the Bible?

This question needs moral clarity, historical care and a tone that does not dodge the pain behind the objection.

Practise in the appBack to blog

Short answer: Christians should plainly condemn race-based chattel slavery as sin, while answering carefully because the Bible uses slavery language across different ancient realities.

The Answerable app opens the pressure point directly:

“The Bible talks about slavery. How can I trust it morally if it doesn’t just condemn the whole thing outright?”
Answerable conversation training · “What about slavery in the Bible?”

This is not a question to brush aside. People have used Scripture wickedly to defend evil. Christians should say that plainly.

The app’s first note is moral clarity.

Start with moral clarity

Answerable says:

“Slavery in the Bible must be answered carefully because the word covers different realities, and because humans have used Scripture wickedly to defend evil.”
Answerable practice card · Ethics

That is a helpful opening. It does not pretend the issue is simple. It also does not defend evil because someone once used a Bible verse to justify it.

The app continues:

From the app“The first note should be moral clarity: human beings made in God’s image must never be treated as property.”

Do not give a quick dismissive answer

The app marks this as a weaker move:

“The Old Testament was just cultural, so Christians should not worry too much about those texts.”
Answerable weak-answer warning

That answer avoids the pressure too quickly. If someone has a serious moral concern, “do not worry about it” will sound careless.

A better answer asks what Scripture restricts, exposes and ultimately points toward in a fallen world.

Regulation is not endorsement

One of the app’s strong responses says:

“Regulation is not the same as moral endorsement, but I agree regulation still feels morally difficult.”
Answerable conversation training · strong response

This is a good sentence because it does two things. It makes an important distinction, and it does not pretend the question is emotionally easy.

The Bible’s world included debt-service, household servitude, foreign slavery after war and brutal forms of bondage. The app does not flatten those into one modern category.

What the app says Scripture does

Answerable summarises the Old Testament material this way:

From the app“Old Testament law does not present these as Edenic ideals; it addresses a fallen social world, restrains abuse, condemns kidnapping, requires rest, limits permanent debt-servitude among Israelites, and gives servants legal protections unusual in the ancient world.”

That does not remove every hard question. It gives a careful path through the question without pretending the Bible is a modern policy document.

Christians must condemn chattel slavery

The app says this plainly:

“Christians should plainly condemn race-based chattel slavery as sin. The Gospel does not dehumanize people; it redeems them.”
Answerable practice card · Ethics

That should be part of the answer. No Christian should sound uncertain about the evil of treating people as property.

The app also says Christians who used Scripture to defend man-stealing slavery were contradicting the Bible’s own condemnation of kidnapping and its doctrine of human dignity.

How to say it in a conversation

You might say:

“I agree this is a serious moral question. Christians should condemn chattel slavery clearly. The Bible’s slavery texts need careful handling because the word covers several ancient realities, but that never gives anyone permission to treat people as property.”

That answer is not complete, but it is a better beginning than dodging the question.

Scripture the app highlights

  • “No longer as a slave, but more than a slave, a beloved brother.” — Philemon 1:16
  • “So whatever you wish that others would do to you, do also to them.” — Matthew 7:12
  • “There is neither slave nor free... for you are all one in Christ Jesus.” — Galatians 3:28

FAQ

Should Christians condemn chattel slavery?

Yes. The app says Christians should plainly condemn race-based chattel slavery as sin.

Does regulation mean approval?

No. The app’s strong answer says regulation is not the same as moral endorsement, while still admitting the issue feels difficult.

Why does tone matter here?

Because people may be raising a real moral wound, not just an argument. A careless answer can do harm.
