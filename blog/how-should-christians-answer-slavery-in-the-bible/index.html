How should Christians answer slavery in the Bible?

This question needs moral clarity, historical care and a tone that does not dodge the pain behind the objection.

Practise in the appBack to blog

Answer in brief: Christians should plainly condemn race-based chattel slavery as sin. The Bible’s language covers different ancient realities, so the answer needs care. But no Christian should sound like they are defending human exploitation.

Answer in brief
  • Begin with moral clarity, not defensiveness.
  • Human beings made in God’s image must never be treated as property.
  • Ancient regulation is not the same as moral endorsement.

This is not a question to brush aside. People have used Scripture wickedly to defend evil. Christians should say that plainly before moving into history or context.

The public-safe Answerable source says this topic must be answered carefully because the word covers different realities and because the moral concern is serious. It also belongs with other hard apologetics questions where tone matters as much as facts.

Start with moral clarity

The first note should not be technical. It should be moral. Human beings made in God’s image must never be treated as property.

If a Christian starts by sounding irritated or defensive, the conversation is already going wrong. The person asking may be raising a real wound, not just trying to score a point.

The Bible’s world was not simple

The Bible’s world included debt-service, household servitude, foreign bondage after war and brutal forms of oppression. The public Answerable source does not flatten these into one modern category.

Old Testament law addressed a fallen social world. It restrained abuse, condemned kidnapping, required rest and gave servants legal protections unusual in the ancient world.

Regulation is not endorsement

Regulating something in a fallen society is not the same as calling it an ideal. That distinction matters. It also does not remove every hard question.

This is why the answer needs both honesty and care. A rushed answer can make it sound as if Christians are defending what they should be condemning. If the conversation moves towards Scripture’s reliability, it may help to read about New Testament copying and textual questions separately.

What not to say

Do not sound like you are minimising evil. Do not defend racial oppression. Do not rush past the moral concern as if the person asking is being unfair.

Also avoid neat answers that make the problem feel small. This is a serious question. It deserves a serious tone.

Scripture to keep in view

  • Exodus 21:16 condemns kidnapping and selling a person.
  • Colossians 4:1 tells masters to act justly and fairly before God.
  • Philemon 16 speaks of receiving Onesimus as more than a bondservant, as a beloved brother.

How to say it gently

You might say, “I agree this is a serious moral question. Christians should condemn chattel slavery clearly. These texts need careful handling, but they never give anyone permission to treat people as property.”

That kind of answer does not dodge the question. It also gives you room to keep listening. You can practise hard ethical questions in Answerable before trying to answer them in person.

FAQ

Should Christians condemn chattel slavery?

Yes. Christians should plainly condemn race-based chattel slavery as sin.

Does regulation mean approval?

No. Regulation in a fallen social world is not the same as moral endorsement.

Why does tone matter here?

Because people may be raising a real moral wound, not just an argument. A careless answer can do harm.
