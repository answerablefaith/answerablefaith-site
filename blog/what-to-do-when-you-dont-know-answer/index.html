What to do when you don’t know the answer

A calm guide for Christians who want to answer honestly without panic, bluffing or losing gentleness.

Practise in the appBack to blog

Answer in brief: When you do not know the answer, do not bluff. Admit it calmly, clarify the real question, promise only what you can follow through on, and come back with a thoughtful answer. Humility is not failure; it is part of faithful witness.

Answer in brief
  • Be honest without sounding defeated.
  • Ask what the person is really asking.
  • Return with a careful answer and a gracious tone.

Most Christians will eventually face a question they cannot answer on the spot. It may be about suffering, the Trinity, the resurrection, Bible reliability or something deeply personal. That moment can feel exposing.

But apologetics is not a performance where every answer must arrive instantly. The goal is to bear witness to Christ with truth and love. Sometimes the most faithful first sentence is, “That is a good question, and I do not want to answer it carelessly.”

Do not pretend you know

A confident guess can do more harm than an honest pause. If you bluff, you may accidentally misrepresent Christianity or make the other person feel handled rather than heard.

It is better to say, “I have not studied that enough to answer well.” That is not weakness. It shows that the question matters, and that truth matters enough not to be rushed.

Clarify the real question

Before searching for an answer, slow the conversation down. Ask, “What part of that troubles you most?” or “Are you asking about evidence, logic, morality or personal experience?”

This matters because the same sentence can hide different concerns. “How can God allow suffering?” may be an argument, a wound, or both. “Did scribes change the New Testament?” may be about manuscripts, trust, or fear that Christian faith rests on fragile ground. A careful answer begins by listening.

Come back with something clear

If you promise to look into it, keep the promise. Do not return with a stack of links or a lecture. Return with one or two clear thoughts, a Scripture passage if appropriate, and an invitation to keep talking.

You might use the Answerable Faith blog to start. For example, there are articles on the Trinity, New Testament copying questions, resurrection hallucination claims and slavery in the Bible.

What not to say

Do not say, “Just have faith,” if you mean, “Stop asking.” Do not say, “That is easy,” when the question is serious. Do not attack the person for raising a hard issue.

Also avoid overpromising. “I will send you a perfect answer” places pressure in the wrong place. “I will look into it and come back carefully” is usually wiser.

How to say it gently

Try this: “I care about that question, and I do not want to give you a shallow answer. Can I think about it and come back to you?”

That answer protects both truth and relationship. It also gives you room to prepare. You can practise in Answerable with model answers, Scripture-led study and a gracious posture before the next conversation.

FAQ

Is it bad if a Christian does not know the answer?

No. Not knowing an answer is not the same as Christianity having no answer. It is better to be honest, listen carefully and return thoughtfully than to bluff.

What should I say when I am stuck?

Say that the question matters and you want to answer it carefully. Then ask a clarifying question and offer to look into it before giving a rushed answer.

Does apologetics mean I need to answer everything instantly?

No. Good apologetics includes patience, humility, careful study and gracious conversation. A rushed answer can be less helpful than an honest pause.

How can a small group practise this?

Use one hard question, practise saying “I do not know yet” without panic, then practise how to research and return with a short, gracious answer.
