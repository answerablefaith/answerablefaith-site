What to do when you don’t know the answer

You do not need to bluff. Honest, calm words can protect both truth and the person in front of you.

Practise in the app Back to blog
Answer in brief

When you do not know the answer, do not bluff. Say so with care. Ask what the person means. Promise only what you can do. Then come back with a short, clear answer.

Every Christian will face a question they cannot answer on the spot. It may be about suffering, the Bible, the Trinity, the resurrection, or a painful life story.

That moment can feel exposing. But it can also be good. You can show that truth matters, people matter, and faith is not a performance.

Say the honest thing

A confident guess can do harm. It may misstate Christian faith. It may also make the other person feel managed, not heard.

Try this instead: “That is a good question. I do not want to answer it badly.” That is not defeat. It shows respect.

Find the real question

Slow the conversation down. Ask, “What part of that troubles you most?” or “Are you asking about evidence, logic, morality, or something personal?”

This helps because one question can hide another. A question about suffering may be an argument. It may also be grief. A question about the Bible may be about facts, trust, or fear.

Come back with something clear

If you say you will look into it, do it. Do not return with a pile of links. Bring one or two clear thoughts. Add a Bible passage if it helps. Then invite the person to keep talking.

The Answerable Faith blog can help you start. You can read about the Trinity, New Testament copying, the resurrection, and slavery in the Bible.

How to say it gently

You might say, “I care about that question. I do not know the best answer yet. Can I think about it and come back to you?”

That keeps the door open. It also gives you time to prepare without pretending.

What not to say

Do not say, “Just have faith,” if you mean, “Stop asking.” Do not say, “That is easy,” when the question is serious.

Do not promise a perfect answer. Say, “I will look into it and come back carefully.” That is usually better.

FAQ

Is it bad if a Christian does not know the answer?

No. Not knowing an answer is not failure. It is better to be honest than to bluff.

What should I say when I am stuck?

Say the question matters. Ask what they mean. Then offer to look into it and come back.

Do Christians need to answer everything at once?

No. Good apologetics includes patience, study, humility and love.

How can a small group practise this?

Choose one hard question. Practise saying, “I do not know yet,” without panic. Then practise a short follow-up answer.
