How do I answer the divine hiddenness argument?

God is not absent, but Christians should answer this objection with both confidence and tenderness.

Practise in the appBack to blog

Answer in brief: The divine hiddenness argument asks why God doesn’t make Himself more obvious. Christianity answers that God has not left Himself without witness: He reveals Himself through creation, conscience, Scripture and supremely through Jesus Christ. But God doesn’t force Himself on people in a way that removes trust, humility and repentance.

Answer in brief
  • Don’t treat the question as silly or merely rebellious.
  • Say clearly that God has given real witness.
  • Keep Jesus, not abstract argument, at the centre.

This question often has two layers. One is intellectual: if God exists, why isn’t His existence undeniable? The other is personal: why does God sometimes feel distant to people who are hurting?

A good Christian answer keeps both layers in view. It doesn’t pretend everyone who asks is cynical. It also doesn’t reduce faith to a demand that God appear on our terms.

God has not left Himself without witness

Christians don’t say God is hidden in the sense that He has given no light. The world points beyond itself. Conscience presses moral truth upon us. Scripture gives God’s words. Most clearly, Jesus reveals the Father.

“God is not hidden in the sense that He has left Himself without witness. He reveals Himself through creation, conscience, Scripture, and supremely through Jesus Christ.”

That matters because the Christian claim is not, “Believe with no reason.” It is that God gives real light, and that light calls for more than curiosity. It calls for trust.

Why doesn’t God remove every doubt?

God could overwhelm us with displays of power. But bare undeniability is not the same as restored relationship. Scripture says the problem is not only lack of information; it is also the human heart. We can suppress, resist or reinterpret the light we already have.

This doesn’t mean every doubter is pretending. Some people genuinely ache over God’s silence. The Bible includes prayers from people who feel forgotten, so Christians should never mock that pain.

The clearest answer is Jesus

The Christian answer reaches its centre in Christ. God has made Himself known not merely by giving signs in the sky, but by coming near in the crucified and risen Son. Jesus enters our darkness, bears sin and brings His people to God.

So the answer is not cold. It says, “Look at Christ.” If you want to know what God is like, don’t start with the strongest feeling of absence. Start with the One who reveals the Father.

How to say it gently

You might say, “I understand why hiddenness feels heavy. Christianity doesn’t say God has given no witness. It says God has made Himself known most clearly in Jesus, while still calling us to trust Him rather than control Him.”

What not to say

Don’t say, “You just don’t want God to exist.” That may sometimes be true of human resistance, but as a first response it sounds dismissive. Start with patience, then explain the difference between honest anguish and demanding God on our terms.

Scripture to keep in view

  • “His invisible attributes... have been clearly perceived.” — Romans 1:20
  • “No one has ever seen God; the only God... He has made Him known.” — John 1:18
  • “You will seek Me and find Me, when you seek Me with all your heart.” — Jeremiah 29:13

FAQ

What is the divine hiddenness argument?

It asks why God does not make Himself more obvious, especially to people who sincerely want to know Him.

Does Christianity say God is completely hidden?

No. Christianity says God gives real witness through creation, conscience, Scripture and supremely through Jesus Christ.

Should Christians mention sin when answering?

Yes, but carefully. Scripture says human resistance matters, but that should not be used to crush someone who is grieving or honestly searching.

Where should the answer end?

With Jesus Christ, because Christianity says God has made Himself known most clearly in the Son.
